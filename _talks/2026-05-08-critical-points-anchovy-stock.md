This poster presentation examines how the state of the north–central Peruvian anchovy stock responds to natural and anthropogenic pressures in a highly variable pelagic ecosystem. The work focuses on identifying critical points, non-linear responses, and potential thresholds associated with changes in stock status.

The central idea is that small pelagic fish populations can respond non-linearly to environmental variability and fishing-related pressures. In systems such as the Peruvian upwelling ecosystem, changes in climate forcing, oceanographic conditions, habitat availability, fishing pressure, and population structure may generate abrupt shifts in stock state rather than gradual responses.

This contribution proposes a stock-state diagnostic perspective based on critical points of pressure–response relationships. The north–central Peruvian anchovy (*Engraulis ringens*) is used as an applied case study to evaluate how natural and anthropogenic drivers may be associated with changes in biological, ecological, and spatial indicators of stock state.

The framework is intended to support ecosystem-informed assessment by identifying potential thresholds, warning signals, and state transitions that can inform adaptive management in small pelagic fish and forage communities.

**Authors:** Elmer Quispe-Salazar; Alvaro Recoba; Jorge Tam

My research tries to make marine systems more measurable, interpretable, and useful for decisions.  
I work with fisheries, biological, oceanographic, and environmental data to understand how marine populations respond to natural variability, fishing pressure, and ecosystem change.

I am particularly interested in the Peruvian anchovy (*Engraulis ringens*) and the Humboldt Current System, one of the most productive marine ecosystems in the world. My work focuses on connecting population dynamics, stock assessment, spatio-temporal ecology, and statistical modelling to support monitoring, assessment, and adaptive fisheries management.

I develop analytical workflows that combine ecological theory, statistical models, and reproducible scientific computing. These workflows include stock indicators, CPUE standardization, acoustic biomass analysis, habitat modelling, pressure–response relationships, and ecosystem-based approaches to fisheries management. My goal is to build tools that are not only technically sound, but also interpretable, auditable, and useful in real scientific and management contexts.

Through [Blue Ecological Systems]({{ base_path }}/services/) and [Pelagytics]({{ base_path }}/software/), I aim to translate scientific methods into practical tools, technical notes, and open workflows for marine ecology, fisheries science, and decision support.

I work across the full analytical chain: from data organization and quality control to model formulation, diagnostics, uncertainty analysis, ecological interpretation, visualization, and communication of results. I am especially interested in methods that help transform complex marine data into quantitative evidence for decision-making.

My current work includes indicators of stock state, natural and anthropogenic pressures, spatio-temporal patterns in pelagic resources, environmental drivers of marine populations, and reproducible workflows in R, Python, Julia, and GitHub.

## Interests

Marine ecosystems are dynamic, complex, and uncertain. Their structure and functioning emerge from interactions among climate variability, oceanographic processes, biological traits, fishing pressure, and human decisions. I am interested in building quantitative approaches that make this complexity more transparent rather than hiding it behind black-box analyses.

I believe that useful fisheries science should connect data, models, uncertainty, and management questions. A model is not only a statistical object; it is a structured hypothesis about how a marine system works. For that reason, I am interested in methods that are interpretable, ecologically grounded, reproducible, and operationally useful.

I am also passionate about scientific computing as a way to improve marine research. Reproducible workflows, open data, version control, automated pipelines, and transparent code can make fisheries science more reliable and easier to update. My long-term interest is to contribute to a more open, quantitative, and internationally connected marine science community.

## About Me

👋 Hey, I'm Elmer.

I am a biologist by training and a quantitative marine ecologist by practice. My work sits between field observations, ecological theory, statistical modelling, and scientific programming. I am based in Peru and work on marine ecosystems, fisheries resources, and quantitative tools for assessment and decision support.

I enjoy building analytical systems that connect data, models, and decisions. I am especially motivated by problems where statistical methods can help clarify ecological mechanisms, detect early signals of change, or improve the way marine resources are assessed and managed.

Outside my technical work, I am interested in continuous learning, scientific communication, open-source tools, international collaboration, and building a stronger research profile in marine quantitative ecology. I am currently strengthening my work in statistical ecology, fisheries modelling, scientific programming, and reproducible research.
